01 · What this is

A forecasting system, tested the way a real planning team would test it

The U.S. Transportation Security Administration publishes the number of passengers screened at its checkpoints every day. It is one of the cleanest public proxies for travel demand that exists: daily resolution, strong weekly rhythm, sharp holiday structure, and seven unbroken years of history.

This project uses that data to answer a practical question: how accurately can next year's daily demand be predicted, and does the forecast hold up when checked against what actually happened? Three models are built, each forecast is made on data the model has never seen, and every result on this page is scored against real actuals — nothing is fitted in hindsight.

Method in one line

Train on 2022–2024 → forecast 2025 → score against real 2025 → refit through 2025 → forecast 2026. The year being predicted is never part of the training data.

03 · The three models

One I built by hand, measured against the two standard tools

Manual decomposition — built from scratch

This is the model I built by hand, without using any forecasting library. The reason is simple: calling a library's .fit() doesn't prove you understand forecasting — it proves you can read documentation. So I built a complete forecaster from first principles, to show I understand what's actually happening underneath the tools.

The idea is that any day's travel demand is the product of a few separate forces, multiplied together:

  • Level — the underlying baseline of how busy travel is overall, and which way it's trending.
  • Annual shape — the slow rise and fall across the year: a deep January lull, the early-summer climb to a peak. Captured as a smooth curve rather than twelve blocky monthly averages.
  • Day of week — Fridays and Sundays are heavy, Tuesdays and Saturdays are light. A repeating weekly fingerprint.
  • Holidays — each federal holiday's own effect, learned from the data.
  • Bridge days — the real insight for travel: the holiday itself is usually a quiet travel day (everyone's already arrived), while the days around it surge. The Sunday after Thanksgiving is the busiest travel day of the year, not Thanksgiving Day. The model learns these surrounding-day patterns explicitly, and even adjusts for which weekday a holiday lands on — because a Monday holiday creates a long weekend that a Wednesday holiday doesn't.

Every piece is inspectable — you can open the model and read exactly why it predicted what it did for any given day. That transparency is the whole point: it's not a black box, it's a model whose reasoning can be explained, checked, and defended.

The test that mattered: this hand-built model, put head-to-head against the two tools the industry actually reaches for, matched both — and on the 2025 validation year, slightly beat Prophet.

Prophet — the modern benchmark

Prophet is the forecasting library most teams reach for today (originally built by Facebook). Given the full U.S. holiday calendar, it handles trend, weekly and yearly patterns, and holidays automatically. It's the strong, modern tool my hand-built model had to stand up against.

SARIMA — the classical benchmark

SARIMA is the traditional statistical workhorse for time series. Here it's set up to capture the weekly rhythm but not the yearly one — included deliberately to show what a model misses when it isn't holiday- and season-aware. It's the baseline that makes the other two look as good as they are.

04 · Planning under a locked horizon

You can't re-plan a month you're already in

A real planning team can't rewrite February's staffing once February has started. So the 2026 forecast runs two ways. The locked annual forecast is made once, from data through end-2025, and frozen for the whole year. The refreshed forecast retrains as each month's actuals close — but with a one-month lock-out: data through end of January can only revise March onward, because February is already underway.

So January and February show three locked forecasts against actual. From March, the refreshed lines (manual and Prophet) appear alongside them — letting you see directly whether retraining on fresh data helps.

Why SARIMA isn't in the refreshed lines

Refit on a short window, SARIMA collapses downward — its bias swings from +12.9% to −24%, worse than leaving it frozen. That fragility is a finding in itself: classical models need careful retraining, so it's kept out of the refreshed panels and flagged in the comparison below.

Through April and May the forecasts sit slightly above actual. That's not a glitch — 2026 demand flattened against the growth the models learned from 2022–2025. No model trained on history predicts a trend break the moment it happens; catching it is the job of the refresh and the monitoring, which is exactly why both exist.

08 · What this shows

What the system achieves

A working forecasting system, accurate to roughly 95% on data it had never seen.

~95% daily accuracy · ~4.5% error · validated on a full held-out year and a live one

What this project demonstrates is a complete forecasting system, built and tested end to end on real public data. Across a full held-out year (2025) and the live months of 2026, the forecasts land within about 5% of actual demand on an average day — the level the contact-center industry treats as the standard for good forecasting. The model I built by hand reached that bar alongside the best off-the-shelf tool available, and stays fully transparent while doing it.

Just as important is what the system gets wrong, and that it can tell you so. Through spring 2026 the forecasts run slightly high, because real demand flattened against the growth seen in prior years — and no forecast trained on history can see a trend break the moment it arrives. The value is that the monitoring and the monthly refresh catch it: as soon as the new lower actuals arrive, the refreshed forecast pulls itself back into line. That self-correction is what separates a forecast you can trust in production from one you can't.

The honest takeaways:

  • The system forecasts daily demand at industry-standard accuracy on data it never trained on.
  • A model built from scratch held its own against the standard tools — proving the method is understood, not just imported.
  • Refreshing the forecast each month, under a realistic locked horizon, measurably improves it.
  • Watching the direction of the error, not just its size, is what flags a demand shift early — the difference between a forecast that reacts and one that drifts.
